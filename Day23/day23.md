# Day 23 – Customer Discovery & MVP Blueprint

## Objective

The objective of Day 23 was to understand how customer discovery and MVP planning can help turn a startup idea into a focused product that solves real customer problems.

For this task, I continued working on my startup project, **Circuit.IQ – AI-Powered Virtual Physics Lab**, using the Startup Validation Report from Day 22. The goal was to identify the ideal customers, understand their pain points, define the right MVP, prioritize features, analyze risks, and create a focused 30-day action plan.

---

## What is Customer Discovery?

Customer discovery is the process of understanding who your real customers are, what problems they face, how serious those problems are, and whether they are willing to use or pay for a solution.

Instead of building a product based only on assumptions, customer discovery involves talking directly to potential users and decision-makers, understanding their needs, and validating whether the proposed solution provides enough value.

For Circuit.IQ, customer discovery means speaking with school principals, HODs, teachers, and educational institutions to understand their practical laboratory challenges and determine whether they would actually use or pay for a virtual physics lab.

---

## Why Customer Discovery Matters

Customer discovery is important because a technically strong product does not automatically become a successful startup.

A startup needs evidence that real customers:

- Experience the problem being solved
- Consider the problem important
- Are interested in the proposed solution
- Can use the product effectively
- Are willing to adopt or pay for it

For Circuit.IQ, the current challenge is not simply adding more features. The main challenge is validating institutional demand through customer interviews and real-world pilots.

The report identified that Circuit.IQ already has a technically strong MVP, but direct customer validation is still missing.

---

## Circuit.IQ Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

The Ideal Customer Profile for Circuit.IQ includes:

### Institution Type

- Private CBSE/ICSE schools for Classes 9–12
- Tier 2 and Tier 3 engineering colleges
- Polytechnic institutes

### Geography – Phase 1

- Jammu & Kashmir
- Delhi NCR
- Selected metro clusters such as Bangalore, Pune, and Hyderabad

### Institution Size

The ideal institution has approximately **300–2,000 students** and experiences pressure related to physical laboratory infrastructure while still having some budget available for digital tools.

### Budget Authority

The primary decision-makers are:

- Principal or Director in schools
- HOD or Dean Academics in colleges

### Trigger Conditions

Potential customers may become interested in Circuit.IQ because of:

- NEP 2020 compliance requirements
- Shortage of laboratory equipment
- Poor practical examination performance
- Need for improved digital learning
- Smart-classroom or smart-lab branding

### Buying Behavior

The ideal customer is likely to prefer a **pilot-first approach**, where the institution can test the platform before making a larger commitment.

---

## Buyer Persona

### Primary Buyer Persona

**Ritu Sharma – School Principal at a Private CBSE School in a Tier-2 City**

Her main goals include:

- Improving student practical and board examination performance
- Supporting NEP 2020 compliance
- Providing better practical learning opportunities
- Improving the institution's smart-lab credentials

Her main frustrations include:

- Limited annual budgets
- Expensive physical laboratory equipment
- Skepticism toward unproven EdTech products
- Limited time for complicated technology onboarding

Her main decision criteria include:

- Free or low-cost pilot
- Clear evidence of educational outcomes
- Simple onboarding
- Minimal teacher training requirements

Secondary personas include an Engineering HOD who needs syllabus mapping and accreditation analytics, and Class 11 students who would use Circuit.IQ through an institutional license.

---

## Top Customer Pain Points

The report identified the following major customer pain points:

1. Physical laboratory equipment may be scarce, shared, or damaged.
2. Existing online learning content is often theory-heavy with limited interactivity.
3. Institutional budget cycles and procurement processes make institutions risk-averse.
4. Free tools such as OLabs and PhET may lack advanced adaptivity, gamification, and modern user experience.
5. Teachers may lack tools to track individual student concept gaps during practical learning.
6. Heavy 3D applications may not work effectively in rural or low-bandwidth environments.
7. As a new platform, Circuit.IQ currently lacks institutional proof points and established brand trust.
8. Institutional sales cycles can take 2–6 months.
9. Educational content must align with both school and engineering curricula.
10. Data privacy and protection of student information, especially for minors, must be considered.

---

## Customer Journey

The proposed customer journey follows four stages:

### 1. Awareness

Potential customers discover Circuit.IQ through:

- LinkedIn content
- Referrals
- Direct outreach
- Email
- Personal and professional networks

### 2. Consideration

Interested institutions receive:

- A frictionless live demonstration
- No-install access
- Support for low-end devices
- A 4–8 week pilot for one or two classes

### 3. Purchase

After a successful pilot, the institution can move toward a simple annual subscription or purchase process designed to work with Indian institutional procurement systems.

### 4. Retention

Circuit.IQ can retain institutions through:

- Teacher training and support
- Regular usage reminders
- Continued product improvements
- Outcome tracking
- Case studies
- Referral programs

---

## Key Buying Triggers

The main buying triggers identified were:

- NEP 2020 compliance pressure for practical and digital learning
- Poor practical examination results
- Accreditation feedback identifying weak laboratory exposure
- Laboratory equipment shortages or damage
- Difficulty scheduling physical laboratory sessions
- Competitive pressure when other institutions adopt smart-lab technology
- New academic year and admission-cycle budget windows

---

## Key Customer Objections

### "We already use OLabs or PhET for free."

Circuit.IQ should position itself as a complementary and more personalized platform offering features such as AI-powered feedback, gamification, and teacher analytics.

### "We don't have a budget approved this year."

A free or low-cost pilot can allow the institution to evaluate the product before making a financial commitment.

### "How do we know it improves student outcomes?"

Circuit.IQ needs real pilot data showing measurable before-and-after outcomes.

### "Our internet or device infrastructure is limited."

A lightweight and low-bandwidth version of Circuit.IQ should be provided.

### "You are a student founder without an established track record."

The best response is to use successful pilot results, measurable outcomes, and case studies to gradually establish credibility.

---

## MVP Recommendation

The MVP should focus on validating whether educational institutions actually need and will adopt Circuit.IQ.

The main objective should be to make the platform easy to demonstrate, easy to test, and capable of generating measurable results during institutional pilots.

The MVP should not attempt to become a complete virtual STEM education platform before customer demand has been validated.

---

## What Should Be Built First

The report recommends prioritizing:

- A frictionless no-install demo or trial link
- A genuinely low-bandwidth mode
- One or two flagship physics simulations
- Simulations mapped to Classes 11–12 and first-year engineering curricula
- A simple teacher usage dashboard
- Basic pilot usage tracking
- Pre/post concept quizzes to measure learning outcomes

These features directly support customer validation and institutional pilots.

---

## What Should Not Be Built Yet

The following features should not be prioritized before obtaining pilot evidence:

- Full STEM suite covering chemistry and biology
- Complex 3D or immersive virtual laboratories
- Advanced LMS integrations
- Enterprise contract management tools
- Native mobile application
- Large-scale API integrations

These features may be useful later, but they do not directly solve the current validation problem.

---

## Success Metrics

The initial MVP success metrics are:

- Complete **8–12 institutional customer interviews**
- Secure at least **1 signed or committed pilot**
- Measure student engagement during the pilot
- Measure pre/post concept-quiz improvement
- Obtain at least **1 verbal or written willingness-to-pay signal**

These metrics will provide stronger evidence of real customer demand.

---

- Low-bandwidth mode
- Class 11–12 physics simulations
- First-year engineering physics simulations
- Basic teacher dashboard
- Pilot usage tracking

### Should Have

- Pre/post concept quizzes
- Simple institutional invoicing
- WhatsApp-friendly reports
- Excel-friendly reports

### Could Have

- Gamification and badges
- Advanced AI misconception detection
- Multi-language user interface

### Won't Have for Now

- Chemistry modules
- Biology modules
- Full immersive 3D laboratories
- LMS/API integrations
- Native mobile application

This prioritization helps Circuit.IQ focus development effort on features that directly support customer validation.

## Pricing Hypothesis

The proposed pricing strategy positions Circuit.IQ between free platforms such as OLabs and PhET and expensive international platforms such as Labster.

The report suggests an approximate **₹25,000 average annual institutional license** as an initial pricing anchor.

The proposed structure is:

**Free 4–8 week pilot → Annual institutional subscription → Optional analytics and accreditation reporting add-ons**

A per-institution pricing model may be easier for educational institutions than per-student pricing and could reduce procurement friction.

This is only a pricing hypothesis and must be validated through customer interviews and real institutional discussions.

---

## Top 5 Risks

### 1. Long Institutional Sales Cycles

**Likelihood:** High  
**Impact:** High

Institutional sales cycles may take several months and could slow startup momentum.

### 2. Free Alternatives

**Likelihood:** High  
**Impact:** High

Free alternatives such as OLabs may reduce customers' willingness to pay for Circuit.IQ.

### 3. No Dedicated Sales or Business Development Resource

**Likelihood:** Medium-High  
**Impact:** Medium-High

Managing development, sales, customer interviews, and institutional relationships may be difficult without dedicated business development support.

### 4. Content Breadth Gap

**Likelihood:** Medium  
**Impact:** Medium

Circuit.IQ currently cannot match the content breadth of established platforms such as Labster or PhET.

### 5. Data Privacy and Child Data Compliance

**Likelihood:** Low-Medium  
**Impact:** Medium-High

Circuit.IQ must consider appropriate data privacy and child-data compliance requirements when working with schools and students.

## 30-Day MVP Plan

### Week 1 – Customer Outreach Preparation

- Create a structured interview script.
- Build a target list of 30–40 schools and colleges.
- Start with institutions in Jammu & Kashmir.
- Begin outreach through LinkedIn, email, and personal networks.

### Week 2 – Customer Interviews

- Complete 8–12 structured interviews.
- Interview principals, HODs, and teachers.
- Record customer pain points.
- Document objections.
- Ask about willingness to pay.

### Week 3 – Pilot Acquisition

- Identify the 2–3 most promising institutions.
- Offer a free 4–8 week pilot.
- Limit the pilot to one or two classes.
- Clearly define pilot success metrics.

### Week 4 – Pilot Preparation and Onboarding

- Onboard teachers and students.
- Set up usage tracking.
- Implement a simple pre/post concept quiz.
- Schedule a mid-pilot review.

### Guiding Principle

**No new product development should be prioritized during this period unless it directly helps secure or successfully run the pilot.**

The current constraint is customer proof, not the number of features.

## Final Verdict

Circuit.IQ demonstrates strong technical execution and addresses a structurally validated problem. However, there is currently no direct customer proof.

To move toward a **Strong Demand Signal**, the next targets are:

- Complete 8–12 customer interviews
- Secure at least one committed institutional pilot

## Biggest Insight

My biggest insight from this activity was that having a technically strong product is not enough to prove that a startup will succeed.

I initially focused heavily on developing Circuit.IQ and adding features. This analysis showed me that the most important next step is not building more features but talking to real customers, understanding their problems, running institutional pilots, and collecting measurable evidence.

The biggest challenge for Circuit.IQ is currently **customer proof, not product development**.
