# Day 31 – AI Supply Chain Control Tower

## 60 Days Claude Challenge

## Overview

On Day 31 of the #60DayClaudeChallenge, I built and tested an **AI Supply Chain Control Tower**, an interactive browser-based simulation that places the user in the role of a **Head of Operations**.

The objective of the simulation was to monitor real-time supply chain disruptions, prioritize operational alerts, select corrective actions, and understand how each decision affects key business performance indicators.

The simulation helped me understand how supply chain leaders must make fast decisions while balancing customer satisfaction, inventory availability, transportation efficiency, operating costs, and revenue protection.

---

## What is a Supply Chain Control Tower?

A Supply Chain Control Tower is a centralized system that provides visibility into different parts of a supply chain.

It helps organizations monitor areas such as:

- Inventory
- Suppliers
- Warehouses
- Transportation
- Shipments
- Customer deliveries
- Operational disruptions

The purpose of a control tower is to help operations teams identify problems early and respond before those problems create larger disruptions.

In this simulation, the control tower presented operational alerts that required quick decisions. Every decision affected different business KPIs.

---

## Why Real-Time Supply Chain Visibility Matters

Real-time visibility is important because supply chain problems can quickly affect multiple areas of a business.

For example, a supplier delay may reduce inventory availability. Low inventory can then affect service levels, which may eventually reduce customer satisfaction.

Real-time monitoring allows operations teams to:

- Detect disruptions quickly
- Identify critical incidents
- Prioritize urgent problems
- Take corrective action
- Reduce operational risks
- Protect customer deliveries
- Control unnecessary costs
- Protect business revenue

The simulation demonstrated that visibility alone is not enough. The operations team must also understand which problem requires immediate attention and which corrective action creates the best outcome.

## What Operational Decision-Making Means

Operational decision-making means making practical decisions that directly affect day-to-day business operations.

In supply chain management, this may involve deciding whether to:

- Expedite a shipment
- Activate a backup supplier
- Reroute transportation
- Increase production
- Transfer inventory
- Approve air freight
- Delay a decision
- Ignore a lower-priority issue

Every decision involves trade-offs.

For example, approving air freight may improve delivery speed but significantly increase operating costs. Similarly, delaying an important decision may save money temporarily but create larger service and inventory problems later.

## How I Prioritized Multiple Alerts

As the simulation progressed, multiple alerts appeared simultaneously.

I prioritized alerts based on:

1. Priority level
2. Remaining response time
3. Potential business impact
4. KPIs affected
5. Risk of the problem becoming worse

I focused first on **Critical** alerts, followed by **High** and **Medium** priority incidents.

This approach helped me understand the importance of operational triage when multiple disruptions occur at the same time.

## My Experience

Using the AI Supply Chain Control Tower was an interesting way to learn supply chain operations through practical decision-making.

Instead of only reading about supply chain concepts, I had to respond to disruptions while monitoring multiple KPIs simultaneously.

As the number of alerts increased, the simulation became more challenging because I had to quickly decide which incidents were most urgent.

The experience showed me that operational management is not simply about solving every problem. It is about solving the **right problem at the right time with the right response**.

---

## Biggest Insight

My biggest insight from this project was that supply chain problems are highly interconnected.

A single supplier, transportation, warehouse, or production disruption can affect multiple areas of a business.

For example:

**Disruption → Inventory Problem → Lower Service Level → Delivery Delays → Lower Customer Satisfaction → Revenue Risk**

This showed me why modern organizations need real-time visibility and structured operational decision-making.
