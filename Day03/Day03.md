## Launching an AI SaaS Startup as a Founder

Been there. Here's what actually works:



### 1. Start With a Problem, Not a Product

The biggest mistake student founders make is building something cool and then looking for customers. Flip it.

**Your unfair advantages as a student:**
- You're *inside* your target market (education, research, student workflows)
- You have access to professors, labs, and other students who will give you honest feedback
- Low overhead means you can survive on tiny revenue early on

Pick a problem you personally face or see constantly. AI tools work best when they replace a specific, painful, repetitive task — not when they're "an AI assistant for everything."



### 2. Validate Before You Build

Spend 2–3 weeks doing this *before* writing a single line of code:

- **Talk to 20 people** who have the problem. Not "would you use this?" — ask "how do you solve this today? What does it cost you in time/money?"
- **Build a fake door**: A landing page with a waitlist or a "Book a Demo" button. Drive traffic via Reddit, LinkedIn, or your university network. If nobody signs up, the problem isn't painful enough.
- **Manual concierge MVP**: Do the thing *manually* using GPT/Claude behind the scenes. If people pay for the manual version, they'll pay for the automated one.

> One of my early companies sold 10 annual contracts before the product existed. The conversations *became* the product spec.



### 3. Finding Your First Customers

**For B2B AI SaaS (recommended for students):**

- **Start hyper-niche**: "AI for real estate agents writing listing descriptions" beats "AI writing assistant." Niche = easier to find customers, easier to market, easier to charge more.
- **LinkedIn outreach**: 50 targeted DMs/week to your ICP (ideal customer profile). Not spammy blasts — personalized, problem-focused messages.
- **Communities first**: Find where your customer hangs out (Slack groups, Discord, subreddits, Facebook groups) and provide value *before* selling.
- **University resources**: Pitch competitions, startup incubators, professor networks — these are underused goldmines.

**Your first 10 customers should come from conversations, not ads.**



### 4. Pricing (Most Students Get This Wrong)

Charge real money from day one. Here's a proven starting framework for AI SaaS:

| Tier | Price | Who It's For |
|------|-------|--------------|
| Starter | $29–$49/mo | Individual/freelancer |
| Pro | $99–$199/mo | Power user or small team |
| Business | $299–$499/mo | Teams, more usage/seats |

**Rules:**
- **Never start free.** Free users give bad feedback, churn instantly, and don't value your product. A $29 plan filters serious users.
- **Annual plans = survival.** Offer 2 months free for annual upfront. Cash in hand beats MRR when you're bootstrapped.
- **Charge based on value, not cost.** If your tool saves a customer $2,000/month, charging $99 is a no-brainer for them.
- **Raise prices faster than you're comfortable with.** You're almost certainly undercharging.


### 5. Building Sustainably on a Student Budget

**Tech stack that won't bankrupt you:**
- Use Claude or OpenAI APIs — don't train your own models (yet)
- Vercel + Supabase + Stripe = full SaaS infrastructure for ~$0–20/month until you scale
- Build only what customers pay for, nothing else

**The unit economics you must track from day 1:**
- **MRR** (Monthly Recurring Revenue)
- **Churn rate** — if >5% monthly, fix retention before acquiring new users
- **CAC vs LTV** — your customer lifetime value must be 3x+ your acquisition cost

**Avoid these traps:**
- Rebuilding what exists (focus on the AI-specific value layer)
- Hiring too early
- Building mobile apps before validating web
- Spending on ads before you have a working sales conversation



### 6. Your 90-Day Plan

| Week | Focus |
|------|-------|
| 1–2 | Talk to 20 potential customers. Define the problem precisely. |
| 3–4 | Launch a landing page. Get 50+ waitlist signups or kill the idea. |
| 5–8 | Build the concierge MVP. Get 3–5 paying customers manually. |
| 9–12 | Automate what customers are paying for. Launch v1. Hit $500 MRR. |

$500 MRR in 90 days is a realistic, meaningful goal. It proves the model before you go deeper.



The single biggest advantage you have right now is **low personal burn rate**. You can operate lean, experiment fast, and take risks a 30-year-old with a mortgage can't. Use that window aggressively — but validate ruthlessly before you build.

Here's a practical, honest roadmap for building an AI SaaS as a student — prioritized by what actually matters at each stage.Here's how to think through each phase properly.



**Phase 1 — Validate before you build anything**

The mistake almost every student makes is jumping straight into code. Spend two weeks first. Build a landing page (a single HTML file on Vercel is fine), write down your hypothesis ("people who do X struggle with Y and would pay for Z"), and talk to 10 real humans. If you can't get 10 people to spend 30 minutes talking about their problem, the market probably isn't there.



**Phase 2 — The right MVP stack**

For an AI SaaS MVP, this combo is unbeatable as a student:

- **Frontend/backend**: Next.js with the App Router. It handles both the UI and your API routes in one repo. Deploy it with a single `git push`.
- **Database + auth**: Supabase. It gives you Postgres, Row Level Security, and social login out of the box for free. Don't build your own auth — ever.
- **AI layer**: Start with the Anthropic SDK (or OpenAI's, depending on your use case). Use streaming responses so users see output immediately rather than waiting. Keep your prompts in a separate file so you can iterate them without touching business logic.
- **Payments**: Stripe. Set it up from day one, even if you're giving free access. You want the plumbing ready before you need it.
- **Hosting**: Vercel free tier handles everything until you hit serious traffic.

Your AI integration pattern should look like: user input → server-side validation → prompt construction → streaming API call → streamed response back to client. Never call the AI API directly from the browser — your API key will get exposed.



**Phase 3 — Deploy and find beta users**

Ship to 50 real users before you write another feature. Post in Discord communities, Reddit, LinkedIn, and your university Slack. Offer lifetime deals to early users in exchange for feedback. Use PostHog (free tier) for analytics so you can see where people drop off.

The most important engineering work at this stage isn't features — it's observability. Log every AI request with the input, output, latency, and token count. You need this data to understand what's costing you money and what's actually working.



**Phase 4 — Scale only what breaks**

Don't over-engineer early. But when you get real users, three things will cause pain:

- **AI costs**: implement per-user token budgets immediately. A few users can accidentally bankrupt you with heavy usage. Use Redis (Upstash has a generous free tier) to track and rate-limit usage.
- **Latency**: cache responses for identical or near-identical inputs. For anything that doesn't need to be real-time, move it to a background queue (Inngest or trigger.dev work well with Vercel).
- **Auth and data isolation**: make sure Row Level Security in Supabase is locked down. Each user must only ever see their own data. Test this explicitly.



**Phase 5 — Monetize with usage-based billing**

Usage-based pricing fits AI products naturally because your costs scale with usage. Stripe's metered billing handles this. A common pattern: free tier with a token/request cap, then a flat monthly subscription for higher limits, then an enterprise tier for teams.

One practical tip — set your pricing higher than feels comfortable. Students tend to underprice dramatically. If someone is getting real value from your tool, $20–50/month is not unreasonable.



**The thing that actually matters most**

Build the simplest version that does one thing really well, get it in front of real users in under 6 weeks, and let their feedback dictate everything after that. # Day 3 – Role-Based Prompting

## Objective

Today's goal was to understand how assigning different roles to Claude changes the quality and perspective of its responses.



## What I Did Today

I explored the concept of **Role-Based Prompting** by asking Claude the same question in three different ways.

First, I asked the question without assigning any role. Then I asked the same question again, but this time I told Claude to act as a startup founder. Finally, I asked it to respond as a senior software developer.

Even though the question stayed the same, each response was completely different because Claude answered from the perspective of the assigned role.



## What is Role-Based Prompting?

Role-Based Prompting means telling the AI who it should act as before answering your question. Instead of giving a general response, the AI thinks like that specific professional and provides advice based on their experience and priorities.

For example, you can ask Claude to act as a Founder, Developer, Product Manager, HR Manager, Marketer, Consultant, or any other expert depending on the type of answer you need.



## Prompts I Used

### Prompt 1 – Without a Role

```text
How can I launch an AI SaaS startup as a student?
```

### Prompt 2 – Founder Role

```text
Act as a startup founder with multiple successful SaaS companies.

How can I launch an AI SaaS startup as a student?

Give practical business advice, focus on validation, finding customers, pricing, and building a sustainable business.
```

### Prompt 3 – Developer Role

```text
Act as a senior software engineer with experience building AI SaaS products.

How can I launch an AI SaaS startup as a student?

Focus on the technical roadmap, technologies, MVP development, deployment, APIs, scalability, and AI integration.
```



## Comparison of Responses

| Without Role              | Founder Role                    | Developer Role                       |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| General advice            | Focused on business strategy    | Focused on technical implementation  |
| Generic suggestions       | Market validation and customers | Tech stack and software architecture |
| Simple roadmap            | Revenue and growth planning     | APIs, deployment, scalability        |
| Surface-level explanation | Entrepreneur mindset            | Engineering mindset                  |



## What I Learned

This activity helped me understand that the AI doesn't just change its wording when given a role—it changes the way it approaches the problem.

The Founder focused on solving customer problems, validating ideas, pricing, and building a business.

The Developer focused on choosing technologies, building an MVP, integrating AI models, and deploying the application.

The response without a role was still useful, but it was much more general and lacked the depth of the other two.



## Biggest Observation

The biggest thing I noticed today was that **the quality of the answer depends not only on the question but also on the role you assign to the AI.**

Simply telling Claude who it should act as produced much more focused, practical, and professional responses.
